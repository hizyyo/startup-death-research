# Failure Taxonomy

# The Anatomy of Startup Death

## Startup Failure Taxonomy v1.2

## Purpose

This taxonomy defines the categories used to classify startup failure causes. The goal is to make failed startups comparable across industries, business models, and stages.

## Taxonomy Rules

- Each startup should have one primary failure cause.
- Each startup may have multiple secondary failure causes.
- Causes should be assigned based on evidence, not vibes.
- Specific causes are better than vague causes.
- Product-market fit failure should be used carefully because it can hide more precise causes.
- Fundraising dependency should not be used as the primary cause if the evidence shows a deeper business failure, such as weak retention, high CAC, bad pricing, or no urgent pain.
- If a startup is mainly a mismatch between a scalable software story and a custom/services reality, use business model mismatch.
- If a social, marketplace, or community product fails because abuse, fraud, harassment, moderation, or trust problems destroy usage or growth, use trust and safety failure.

# 1. Distribution Failure

## Definition

The startup could not acquire customers reliably, repeatedly, or cheaply enough.

## Common Symptoms

- Weak inbound demand.
- Paid acquisition did not scale.
- CAC too high.
- No repeatable sales process.
- Founder-led sales did not become scalable GTM.
- Growth depended on one-off launches or personal network.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder says acquisition was too hard.
- Sales pipeline was weak.
- Paid ads were unprofitable.
- Growth stalled after launch.
- Company had product usage but no reliable channel.

---

# 2. Weak Retention

## Definition

Users tried the product but did not keep using it.

## Common Symptoms

- High churn.
- Low activation.
- Low repeat usage.
- Weak habit formation.
- Poor cohort retention.
- Users signed up but did not return.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions churn or retention issues.
- Product had signups but low active usage.
- Growth could not compound.
- Users did not build the product into their workflow.

---

# 3. Bad Pricing

## Definition

The startup could not capture enough value through pricing.

## Common Symptoms

- Users liked the product but would not pay.
- Pricing was too low for the sales motion.
- Pricing was too high for perceived value.
- Freemium users did not convert.
- Revenue per customer could not support costs.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions pricing mistakes.
- Many free users but few paid users.
- Customers negotiated heavily or rejected price.
- Sales effort was too expensive for deal size.

---

# 4. No Urgent Pain

## Definition

The product solved a problem that was interesting but not urgent enough for customers to adopt or pay for.

## Common Symptoms

- Many compliments, few purchases.
- Long sales cycles.
- Low demo-to-close conversion.
- Product seen as nice-to-have.
- Users understood the idea but did not prioritize it.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder says users liked it but did not buy.
- Customer interviews were positive but revenue was weak.
- Adoption required behavior change without strong pain.

---

# 5. Too Broad ICP

## Definition

The startup tried to serve too many customer segments and failed to focus on a specific ideal customer profile.

## Common Symptoms

- Generic positioning.
- Unclear buyer.
- Scattered feature roadmap.
- Different users wanted different products.
- Marketing message was vague.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions lack of focus.
- Product was built for everyone.
- Sales conversations varied too much by segment.
- No clear repeatable customer profile emerged.

---

# 6. Market Too Small

## Definition

The target market was too small to support sustainable growth or venture-scale outcomes.

## Common Symptoms

- Small number of reachable customers.
- Narrow niche with low willingness to pay.
- No path to expansion.
- Revenue ceiling appeared early.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions TAM was too small.
- Company saturated its niche quickly.
- Investors rejected the opportunity because the market was too small.

---

# 7. High CAC / Bad Unit Economics

## Definition

The startup could acquire users or customers, but the economics did not work.

## Common Symptoms

- CAC higher than LTV.
- Heavy reliance on paid ads.
- Margins too low.
- Payback period too long.
- Discounts required to close customers.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions CAC/LTV problem.
- Paid growth worked only while subsidized.
- Revenue did not cover acquisition or service costs.

---

# 8. Founder Conflict

## Definition

The company failed because of internal conflict, team breakdown, or founder misalignment.

## Common Symptoms

- Co-founder disagreement.
- Equity conflict.
- Misaligned commitment.
- Leadership breakdown.
- One founder leaves at a critical stage.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions disagreement or misalignment.
- Team split caused shutdown.
- Product or fundraising stopped because of internal conflict.

---

# 9. Fundraising Dependency

## Definition

The startup depended on future funding and could not survive when capital became unavailable.

## Common Symptoms

- High burn.
- Weak revenue.
- Runway collapse.
- Bridge round failed.
- Growth plan depended on more capital.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder says they failed to raise.
- Shutdown happened after funding market changed.
- Company had users but no path to profitability before runway ended.

---

# 10. Platform Risk

## Definition

The startup depended heavily on another platform and was damaged by platform changes, restrictions, or competition.

## Common Symptoms

- API changes killed product.
- App store policy change.
- Search or social algorithm change.
- Platform copied the feature.
- Dependency on one ecosystem.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions platform policy/API changes.
- Company lost access to distribution or data.
- Core functionality depended on a third-party platform.

---

# 11. Marketplace Liquidity Failure

## Definition

The startup could not solve supply-demand balance in a marketplace.

## Common Symptoms

- Too much supply, not enough demand.
- Too much demand, not enough supply.
- Cold-start problem.
- Poor match quality.
- Marketplace worked in one city or niche but did not scale.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions chicken-and-egg problem.
- Transactions did not happen often enough.
- Marketplace had users but low liquidity.

---

# 12. Operational Complexity

## Definition

The startup could not execute or scale operations efficiently enough.

## Common Symptoms

- Manual processes.
- Low margins.
- Hard logistics.
- Customer support burden.
- Scaling created complexity faster than revenue.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions operations were too hard.
- Business required too much manual work.
- Margins fell as company grew.
- Service quality dropped with scale.

---

# 13. Technical Complexity

## Definition

The startup could not build, maintain, or scale the required technology.

## Common Symptoms

- Product reliability issues.
- Technical debt.
- Product could not meet promised functionality.
- Infrastructure costs too high.
- Development took too long.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions technical limitations.
- Product repeatedly failed to launch.
- Customers left because of reliability or performance issues.

---

# 14. Regulatory Or Legal Pressure

## Definition

The startup was blocked or damaged by legal, compliance, or regulatory constraints.

## Common Symptoms

- Expensive compliance.
- Legal threats.
- Regulatory bans.
- Licensing problems.
- Inability to operate in target market.

## Evidence Indicators

- News or founder source mentions regulatory action.
- Company shutdown tied to compliance cost or legal restrictions.

---

# 15. Timing Problem

## Definition

The startup was too early or too late for its market.

## Common Symptoms

- Market education required too much effort.
- Infrastructure was not ready.
- Customer behavior had not shifted yet.
- Incumbents or later entrants captured the market.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder says market was not ready.
- Similar products succeeded years later.
- Adoption required ecosystem changes outside startup control.

---

# 16. Competition Pressure

## Definition

The startup could not compete against stronger competitors, incumbents, or fast followers.

## Common Symptoms

- Competitors had better distribution.
- Incumbents copied features.
- Customer acquisition became too expensive.
- Product differentiation was weak.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions competition directly.
- Market consolidated around larger players.
- Startup could not differentiate enough.

---

# 17. Trust And Safety Failure

## Definition

The startup failed because trust, safety, abuse, fraud, harassment, moderation, or community quality problems damaged the product, user behavior, retention, growth, or regulatory position.

## Common Symptoms

- Toxic user behavior.
- Harassment or abuse.
- Spam or fraud.
- Users do not trust the product or marketplace.
- Moderation costs become too high.
- Public backlash around safety or misuse.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder or source mentions abuse, fraud, harassment, moderation, or safety problems.
- Users leave because the environment feels unsafe or low quality.
- Growth creates more abuse than value.
- Regulators, press, or platforms pressure the company because of safety issues.

## Note

This category is especially useful for anonymous social apps, community products, marketplaces, and products where user-generated behavior is central to the value proposition.

---

# 18. Sales Cycle Too Long

## Definition

The startup failed because the time required to close, implement, and collect revenue from customers was too long for its runway, team capacity, pricing, or funding model.

## Common Symptoms

- Long enterprise sales cycles.
- Pilots or contracts take months to convert into cash.
- Revenue arrives too late relative to runway.
- Customer approval requires many stakeholders.
- Implementation or procurement delays block growth.
- Sales motion is too slow for the company's burn or financing plan.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder mentions long sales cycles, delayed contracts, pilots that did not convert fast enough, or revenue that arrived too late.
- Company had customer interest or signed deals but could not survive until deployment or payment.
- The economics might work per customer, but the sales timeline does not fit the startup's available runway.

## Note

Use this when the main issue is timing of revenue conversion, not lack of demand. If customers liked the idea but did not care enough to buy, use no urgent pain. If sales effort was too expensive for deal size, use bad pricing or high CAC / bad unit economics.

---

# 19. Business Model Mismatch

## Definition

The startup failed because the actual business model did not match the intended scalable startup model. Common examples include a supposed SaaS becoming a custom services agency, a software company requiring too much manual work, or a venture-backed model that behaves like a low-margin service business.

## Common Symptoms

- Customers require custom implementations.
- Revenue exists but does not scale like software.
- Team spends most time on services or operations.
- Product roadmap becomes customer-specific.
- Gross margins or delivery model do not fit the startup story.
- Sales promise software, but delivery requires consulting.

## Evidence Indicators

- Founder says the company became an agency, consultancy, or services business.
- The product could not be standardized across customers.
- Growth required proportional increases in labor.
- Investors or founders expected software economics but got services economics.

## Note

Use this category when the core issue is not simply operational complexity, but a mismatch between the intended business model and the real delivery model.

---

# 20. Product-Market Fit Failure

## Definition

The startup failed because the product did not satisfy a strong enough market need, and a more specific cause cannot be confidently identified.

## Common Symptoms

- Low adoption.
- Weak retention.
- Weak willingness to pay.
- Unclear target customer.
- No repeatable use case.

## Evidence Indicators

- Source says no product-market fit.
- Evidence is too broad to assign a more specific cause.

## Note

Use this category carefully. It is often better to classify the more specific underlying cause, such as weak retention, bad pricing, no urgent pain, or too broad ICP.

Product-market fit failure should be used as a fallback only when the evidence is too broad to classify a more precise cause.

---

# Special Classification Notes

## Fundraising Dependency

Use fundraising dependency as primary only when the evidence suggests the company might have continued if capital were available and no deeper failure cause is clearly documented.

Use fundraising dependency as secondary when the startup ran out of runway because of another issue, such as high CAC, weak retention, operational complexity, or bad unit economics.

## Product-Market Fit Failure

Do not use product-market fit failure as a shortcut. Prefer more specific causes:

- If users sign up but do not return, use weak retention.
- If users like the product but do not pay, use bad pricing or no urgent pain.
- If the team cannot find customers, use distribution failure.
- If the target customer is unclear, use too broad ICP.
- If the market is too small, use market too small.

## Multi-Cause Failures

Most startups fail from multiple causes. Still, the dataset requires one primary cause so that analysis is possible. Secondary causes should capture the rest of the failure pattern.
