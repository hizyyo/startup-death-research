# Case Study: MyCity

# The Anatomy of Startup Death

## Summary

MyCity was a SaaS platform for Russian municipalities to collect resident feedback and manage citizen communication. It failed because the local government processes required for the product did not exist in most target cities, making it impossible to sell a tool for a workflow that had not been created yet.

## What MyCity Built

MyCity was a feedback and relationship management platform for local governments. Citizens could submit suggestions, complaints, and ideas through the platform. Municipal staff could track, respond, and report on resident feedback.

The product was technically functional. The problem was not the software. The problem was that the organizational process it supported did not exist inside most Russian municipalities.

## What Went Wrong

Founder Stepa Mitaki's Failory interview describes a startup that tried to sell software for a business process that its target customers did not have.

MyCity assumed that municipalities wanted to systematically collect and manage resident feedback. The reality was that most smaller Russian cities did not have a dedicated process, team, or budget for this kind of work. There was no "owner" of resident feedback management because the function did not exist as a defined role.

Three specific problems:

1. **The process did not exist.** MyCity was trying to sell software for a workflow that municipalities had not yet created. This is fundamentally different from selling software that improves an existing, budgeted process. When there is no existing process, there is no budget line, no responsible person, and no urgency to change.

2. **Bottom-up demand could not penetrate.** The founder tried to start with individual employees inside municipalities who could see the value of the product. But without top-down mandate or budget, these employees could not make purchasing decisions. The product was useful to the people who used it, but useful is not the same as funded.

3. **Direct sales to government are slow and unpredictable.** Government sales cycles are long even when the need is clear. When the need is not yet recognized as a priority, the sales cycle becomes indefinite.

## Warning Signs

- Conversations with potential users were positive but did not lead to contracts
- Individual employees liked the product but could not buy it
- The product required organizational change to use effectively
- No municipality had a budget line for resident feedback software

## Why It Matters

MyCity is a useful counterexample to the common advice that "any problem can be a startup."

The case shows that even a genuinely useful product can fail if the market structure does not support its adoption. MyCity's product worked. Municipal employees who used it found it helpful. But the organizational infrastructure required to buy and use the product did not exist.

This is different from a product that fails because the market does not want it. MyCity failed because the market could not adopt it without first creating a process that it did not yet have.

For founders, the lesson is to distinguish between "this is useful" and "this fits an existing budgeted process." Selling to a process that does not exist yet takes longer and costs more than most bootstrapped startups can sustain.
