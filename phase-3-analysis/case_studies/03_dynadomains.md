# Case Study: DynaDomains

# The Anatomy of Startup Death

## Summary

DynaDomains was a marketplace for leasing valuable domain names to businesses. The founder listed over $3M worth of premium domains, spent $4K on ads, and sent 3,500+ direct outreach emails. Zero leases were signed. The marketplace had supply but no demand.

## What DynaDomains Built

DynaDomains was a two-sided marketplace connecting domain name owners with businesses that wanted to lease premium domains. Instead of buying expensive domains outright, businesses could lease them for a monthly fee. Domain owners could generate recurring revenue from domains they were not using.

The economic logic was reasonable: premium domains are expensive to buy, and many sit unused. Leasing creates liquidity for owners and affordability for tenants. But the marketplace never found enough tenants.

## What Went Wrong

Founder Timothy Carambat's Indie Hackers postmortem provides unusually concrete data about why the marketplace failed.

The supply side was not the problem. DynaDomains listed over $3M worth of premium domains. Domain owners were willing to lease their assets.

The demand side was the problem. Businesses simply were not looking to lease domain names.

Three specific failures:

1. **Zero demand from paid acquisition.** The founder spent $4,000 on ads targeting businesses that might want premium domains. The ads generated zero leases. Not one. This is unusually clean evidence of a demand problem because the acquisition cost was measured and the result was zero.

2. **Cold outreach did not produce results.** The founder sent over 3,500 direct emails to potential lessees. With that volume of targeted outreach, even a 0.1% conversion rate would have produced 3-4 leases. The fact that zero leases came from ads and outreach combined suggests there was no active demand for domain leasing at any price or messaging.

3. **Domain leasing is a behavior that does not exist.** Businesses either buy domains outright or use a subdomain/alternative name. The category of "domain leasing" is not a recognized buying behavior. DynaDomains was trying to create a market, not serve an existing one.

## Warning Signs

- First ad campaigns produced zero conversions
- Cold email outreach at high volume produced zero responses
- Discovery conversations with potential customers showed interest but no action
- The same domain could be purchased outright for a price that made leasing unattractive

## Why It Matters

DynaDomains is one of the strongest small marketplace cases in the dataset because the founder published exact numbers.

Most marketplace postmortems describe liquidity problems in qualitative terms. DynaDomains provides concrete evidence: $3M in supply, $4K in ads, 3,500+ emails, zero demand.

The case is especially useful for founders considering a marketplace idea. It shows that a marketplace can have excellent supply, logical economics, and reasonable execution — and still fail because the buying behavior does not exist on the other side.

The hardest part of building a marketplace is not attracting supply. It is creating demand where none existed before.
